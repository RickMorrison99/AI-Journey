# Non-Functional Requirements & Telecom AI Governance

!!! note "Audience & Scope"
    This page is written for engineers and architects in the **OSS — Orchestration and Automation (O&A)** team at Rogers Communications. It addresses how to specify, elicit, and enforce Non-Functional Requirements (NFRs) for AI-assisted network automation systems built on YANG/NETCONF/NSO and TM Forum APIs. NFRs are treated as **first-class architectural drivers**, not post-shipping checklists.

---

## 1. Why NFRs Are First-Class Citizens in AI Systems

In traditional software, NFRs — availability, latency, recoverability, security — are often negotiated late in the project lifecycle. In AI systems embedded in live network operations, this pattern is dangerous. An LLM inference call that hangs for 30 seconds inside a NETCONF commit workflow can cascade into a provisioning outage. A zero-touch automation agent that makes opaque routing decisions can violate regulatory obligations without any engineer noticing until after the fact.

The O&A team operates at the intersection of two high-stakes domains:

- **Network reliability**: The automation layer directly influences whether physical network elements (BNGs, RAN controllers, optical transport) behave correctly.
- **AI indeterminism**: Large language models, agentic planners, and embedding-based retrieval systems introduce probabilistic, non-deterministic behaviour into what has historically been a deterministic control plane.

This combination makes NFRs **the primary risk surface for responsible AI in telecom operations**. The responsible AI dimensions already documented on this site — fairness, privacy, environmental impact, security — each translate into concrete, measurable NFRs that must be specified before the first line of AI code is written.

### 1.1 Connecting NFRs to O&A's Responsible AI Dimensions

| Responsible AI Dimension | Corresponding NFR Category | O&A Example |
|---|---|---|
| Algorithmic accountability | Explainability, Auditability | Every NETCONF config change driven by an LLM must log the reasoning chain |
| Privacy | Data minimisation, Access control | Telemetry fed to AI models must be stripped of subscriber PII before inference |
| Security | Adversarial robustness, Least privilege | NSO service templates generated by AI must pass a YANG schema validator before commit |
| Environmental | Energy efficiency | LLM inference workloads must carry a carbon-cost budget per automation job |
| Market / Fairness | Non-discrimination | Traffic engineering recommendations must be auditable for carrier-of-carrier fairness |

NFRs bridge the gap between aspirational responsible AI principles and enforceable engineering constraints. Without them, principles remain posters on a wall.

## 2. Thought Leader & Framework Profiles

### 2.1 Michael Nygard — *Release It!* (2007, 2nd ed. 2018)

Michael Nygard's *Release It! Design and Deploy Production-Ready Software* emerged from post-mortems of large-scale production failures. Nygard's core thesis is that software systems fail in predictable ways — and that engineering teams can build **stability patterns** to contain those failures before they cascade.

Nygard introduced the canonical vocabulary of stability patterns now considered table-stakes in distributed systems:

- **Circuit Breaker**: A finite-state machine that wraps a remote call. When consecutive failures exceed a threshold, the breaker "opens" and returns fast-fail responses, preventing thread exhaustion from accumulating against a degraded downstream.
- **Timeout**: Every integration point must have an explicit timeout. "The network will eventually respond" is a system design flaw in disguise.
- **Bulkhead**: Isolation of resource pools so that a failure in one subsystem cannot consume the thread pool, connection pool, or CPU budget of an unrelated subsystem.
- **Back Pressure**: A mechanism for a downstream service to signal that its capacity is saturated, causing upstream producers to slow or shed load rather than overwhelm the system.
- **Fail Fast**: Validate inputs and preconditions at the earliest possible point. An invalid YANG payload should be rejected at the HTTP gateway, not after a NETCONF session has been opened.
- **Steady State**: Purge data accumulation (logs, metrics, event queues) so that systems do not degrade over time due to unbounded growth.

**Why this matters for O&A AI systems**: LLM inference endpoints exhibit exactly the failure modes Nygard catalogued — they time out under load, they return slowly degraded quality rather than errors, and they can saturate connection pools when called naively in tight automation loops. Nygard's patterns are **directly applicable** as NFRs for any AI inference pipeline integrated into the O&A automation stack.

### 2.2 Len Bass, Paul Clements & Rick Kazman — *Software Architecture in Practice* & the Quality Attribute Workshop

The Bass/Clements/Kazman (BCK) school of software architecture, developed over decades at the Software Engineering Institute (SEI) at Carnegie Mellon University, established the formal concept of **quality attributes as architectural drivers**. The key insight is that functional requirements alone cannot determine architecture — it is the quality attributes (NFRs) that force the hard architectural decisions: whether to use asynchronous messaging, whether to deploy active-active redundancy, whether to introduce a caching layer.

The **Quality Attribute Workshop (QAW)** is a structured facilitation technique for eliciting NFRs from stakeholders before architecture decisions are made. It produces **quality attribute scenarios** — precise, testable statements of system behaviour under specific conditions.

A quality attribute scenario has six parts:

1. **Source** — who or what initiates the stimulus
2. **Stimulus** — the event or condition occurring
3. **Artifact** — the part of the system affected
4. **Environment** — the system's operational state at the time
5. **Response** — what the system does
6. **Response Measure** — a quantifiable outcome used to test whether the response is adequate

BCK's taxonomy of quality attributes maps closely to the ISO/IEC 25010 standard (see Section 6) and includes: availability, interoperability, modifiability, performance, security, testability, and usability.

**Why this matters for O&A**: Network automation systems involve a diverse stakeholder group — NOC operators, network architects, security teams, regulatory compliance, and executive sponsors. The QAW technique surfaces the hidden NFRs that each stakeholder group implicitly holds but never formally states. Skipping this step is a common cause of late-stage architectural rework.

### 2.3 GSMA NG.116 — AI Principles for Telcos

The GSMA's **NG.116 (Operator Platform AI Principles)** document is the industry's most authoritative statement of non-functional AI requirements specific to telecommunications network operators. Published under the GSMA's Network Functions Virtualisation and AI work streams, NG.116 establishes six foundational principles:

1. **Accountability** — AI systems must have identifiable human owners responsible for outcomes.
2. **Transparency & Explainability** — Decisions affecting network behaviour must be interpretable by engineers and, where required, by regulators.
3. **Fairness & Non-Discrimination** — AI-driven traffic management must not systematically disadvantage any class of subscriber or carrier.
4. **Privacy & Security** — AI systems processing network telemetry must comply with privacy law and be hardened against adversarial inputs.
5. **Reliability & Robustness** — AI components must meet the same or stricter availability targets as the network elements they manage.
6. **Energy Efficiency** — AI inference workloads must be proportionate; telcos cannot offset sustainability gains from network automation with runaway ML compute costs.

NG.116 is not a prescriptive technical standard — it is a **principles framework** that national regulators (including the CRTC in Canada) increasingly reference when auditing operator AI deployments. O&A teams building AI-driven automation are well-advised to map their NFRs explicitly to NG.116 principles to support regulatory readiness.

### 2.4 ISO/IEC 25010 — Systems and Software Quality Requirements and Evaluation (SQuaRE)

ISO/IEC 25010 is the international standard for software product quality. It defines a **hierarchical quality model** with eight top-level quality characteristics, each decomposed into sub-characteristics. This taxonomy provides the formal vocabulary for specifying NFRs in a way that is unambiguous, contractually enforceable, and auditable.

The eight top-level characteristics are:

| Characteristic | Key Sub-characteristics |
|---|---|
| Functional Suitability | Completeness, Correctness, Appropriateness |
| Performance Efficiency | Time Behaviour, Resource Utilisation, Capacity |
| Compatibility | Co-existence, Interoperability |
| Usability | Learnability, Operability, User Error Protection |
| Reliability | Maturity, Availability, Fault Tolerance, Recoverability |
| Security | Confidentiality, Integrity, Non-repudiation, Authenticity |
| Maintainability | Modularity, Reusability, Analysability, Modifiability, Testability |
| Portability | Adaptability, Installability, Replaceability |

ISO/IEC 25010 is the backbone of Section 6 of this page, where each characteristic is mapped to concrete O&A AI examples.

## 3. Stability NFRs for AI Pipelines (Nygard Patterns in Practice)

Nygard's patterns translate directly into testable NFRs. This section defines each pattern as an engineering requirement for O&A AI pipelines.

### 3.1 Circuit Breakers for LLM Calls

**NFR**: *Every call from an O&A automation workflow to an LLM inference endpoint MUST be wrapped in a circuit breaker. The breaker MUST open after 5 consecutive failures or a 60-second sliding-window error rate above 20%, and MUST remain open for a minimum of 30 seconds before a probe request is permitted.*

LLM inference services (whether self-hosted vLLM clusters or external APIs) are not simple CRUD services. They can:

- Time out silently under GPU memory pressure
- Return partial responses that are syntactically valid JSON but semantically malformed YANG
- Queue requests indefinitely when rate-limited, consuming caller threads

A circuit breaker prevents these failure modes from propagating into the NETCONF/NSO commit pipeline.

```python
# circuit_breaker.py — pseudocode illustrating a circuit breaker
# wrapping an LLM call within an O&A automation workflow.
# Uses a simplified three-state machine: CLOSED → OPEN → HALF_OPEN

import time
import functools
from enum import Enum, auto

class State(Enum):
    CLOSED    = auto()   # Normal operation: calls pass through
    OPEN      = auto()   # Failure threshold exceeded: fast-fail
    HALF_OPEN = auto()   # Probe state: one call allowed through

class CircuitBreaker:
    def __init__(
        self,
        failure_threshold: int = 5,
        recovery_timeout: float = 30.0,
        expected_exception: type = Exception,
    ):
        self.failure_threshold  = failure_threshold
        self.recovery_timeout   = recovery_timeout
        self.expected_exception = expected_exception
        self._failures          = 0
        self._state             = State.CLOSED
        self._opened_at: float  = 0.0

    @property
    def state(self) -> State:
        if self._state is State.OPEN:
            if time.monotonic() - self._opened_at >= self.recovery_timeout:
                self._state = State.HALF_OPEN
        return self._state

    def call(self, func, *args, **kwargs):
        if self.state is State.OPEN:
            raise RuntimeError(
                "Circuit OPEN — LLM inference endpoint unavailable. "
                "Falling back to deterministic NSO template path."
            )
        try:
            result = func(*args, **kwargs)
            self._on_success()
            return result
        except self.expected_exception as exc:
            self._on_failure()
            raise

    def _on_success(self):
        self._failures = 0
        self._state    = State.CLOSED

    def _on_failure(self):
        self._failures += 1
        if self._failures >= self.failure_threshold:
            self._state    = State.OPEN
            self._opened_at = time.monotonic()


# --- Usage in an O&A NETCONF provisioning workflow ---

llm_breaker = CircuitBreaker(
    failure_threshold=5,
    recovery_timeout=30.0,
)

def generate_yang_config_via_llm(service_order: dict) -> str:
    """Call LLM to generate a YANG-compliant config fragment."""
    # ... actual HTTP call to inference endpoint ...
    pass

def provision_service(service_order: dict) -> None:
    try:
        yang_config = llm_breaker.call(
            generate_yang_config_via_llm,
            service_order,
        )
        # Validate schema before committing to NSO
        validate_yang_schema(yang_config)
        commit_via_netconf(yang_config)
    except RuntimeError as e:
        # Circuit is open — use deterministic fallback template
        log.warning("LLM unavailable: %s. Using static NSO template.", e)
        yang_config = render_static_nso_template(service_order)
        commit_via_netconf(yang_config)
```

!!! warning "Fallback Behaviour Is a Requirement"
    The circuit breaker only solves half the problem. The **fallback path** — what the system does when the breaker is open — must be explicitly specified as an NFR. For O&A, the fallback MUST be a deterministic, human-auditable path (e.g., a versioned NSO service template), never silence or an unhandled exception.

### 3.2 Timeout Budgets for Agentic Workflows

**NFR**: *Each step in an O&A agentic workflow MUST have an explicit timeout. The cumulative end-to-end timeout for any workflow touching a live NETCONF session MUST NOT exceed 10 seconds for read-only operations and 30 seconds for configuration commits.*

Agentic AI workflows (multi-step LLM reasoning, tool-calling loops, RAG retrieval chains) introduce a new class of latency risk: the system can legitimately be "doing useful work" for an extended period, making it hard to distinguish productive computation from a hung process. Timeout budgets must be defined per workflow step, not just at the top level.

| Workflow Step | Recommended Timeout | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| RAG document retrieval | 2 s | Vector DB queries should be sub-second |
| LLM prompt construction | 500 ms | CPU-bound; no I/O |
| LLM inference (single call) | 8 s | P99 for 2k-token responses on hosted endpoints |
| YANG schema validation | 1 s | Local; deterministic |
| NSO dry-run commit | 5 s | Network I/O to NSO; should be fast |
| NETCONF live commit | 15 s | Includes device round-trip; bounded by slowest element |
| TM Forum API callback | 3 s | Async notification; receiver must be responsive |

---

### 3.3 Bulkhead Isolation Between Automation Domains

**NFR**: *AI inference resources (thread pools, connection pools, async task queues) MUST be partitioned by automation domain. Failures in the IP/MPLS domain's AI pipeline MUST NOT exhaust resources used by the RAN automation or optical transport pipelines.*

Bulkhead partitioning in O&A means:

- Separate Celery queues (or equivalent) per network domain
- Separate connection pools for LLM endpoints per domain priority tier
- Resource limits (CPU, memory, concurrent NETCONF sessions) enforced at the container level via Kubernetes `ResourceQuota`

Without bulkheads, a burst of AI-assisted IP/MPLS reconfigurations during a traffic incident can starve the RAN automation pipeline of inference capacity at the exact moment it is needed most.

---

### 3.4 Back Pressure When Event Queues Fill

**NFR**: *O&A event-driven pipelines MUST implement back pressure. When the pending-event queue depth exceeds a configurable high-water mark (default: 1,000 events), the system MUST apply one of: (a) rate limiting on inbound TM Forum API callbacks, (b) circuit-breaking of new AI inference requests, or (c) shedding of low-priority telemetry events.*

Back pressure is the mechanism that prevents a fast producer (network telemetry, TM Forum event streams) from overwhelming a slow consumer (LLM inference, NETCONF commit queue). In O&A, this is especially relevant during network incidents when telemetry volume spikes by orders of magnitude at the exact moment the automation system is under heaviest load.

## 4. Eliciting NFRs: Quality Attribute Workshops (Bass / BCK)

### 4.1 The QAW Process

The Quality Attribute Workshop, as defined by the SEI, runs through seven steps:

1. **Presentation** — System mission, constraints, and architecture overview presented to stakeholders
2. **Business/Mission Presentation** — Stakeholders describe operational priorities
3. **Architectural Plan Presentation** — Current or planned architecture described
4. **Identification of Architectural Drivers** — Facilitator surfaces the handful of NFRs that will most constrain the architecture
5. **Scenario Brainstorm** — Stakeholders generate raw quality attribute scenarios (no filtering yet)
6. **Scenario Consolidation** — Duplicate/overlapping scenarios merged
7. **Scenario Prioritisation** — Stakeholders vote; top scenarios become NFR backlog items

For O&A, the key stakeholder groups to include in a QAW are: **NOC operators** (availability, recoverability), **network architects** (performance, modifiability), **security/compliance** (security, auditability), **platform engineering** (deployability, testability), and **regulatory/legal** (explainability, non-repudiation).

### 4.2 Quality Attribute Scenario Structure

Each scenario produced by the QAW is expressed in the six-part structure described in Section 2.2. Below are example scenarios for O&A AI systems:

| # | Source | Stimulus | Artifact | Environment | Response | Response Measure |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| QAS-01 | NOC operator | Requests AI-generated root-cause analysis for a BGP flap | LLM reasoning engine + NSO inventory | Peak hours, 80% inference capacity utilised | System returns structured RCA with YANG diff and confidence score | Response delivered within 5 s; confidence score ≥ 0.75 |
| QAS-02 | Automated TM Forum event | L3VPN service-order received | AI provisioning pipeline | Normal operations | YANG config generated, validated, committed to NSO | End-to-end in < 30 s; zero invalid NETCONF commits |
| QAS-03 | LLM inference endpoint | Returns HTTP 503 | Circuit breaker + provisioning workflow | Degraded (inference cluster at capacity) | Circuit opens; workflow falls back to NSO static template | Fallback completes within 5 s; alert raised to on-call |
| QAS-04 | Regulatory auditor | Requests explanation of last 90 days of AI-driven config changes | Audit log / explainability store | Post-incident review | System produces structured audit trail with per-decision rationale | 100% of AI-driven commits have a linked reasoning artefact |
| QAS-05 | Adversarial input (prompt injection) | Malformed service-order description designed to override AI instructions | AI input validation layer | Normal operations | Input rejected before LLM call; alert raised | Zero prompt-injection attempts reach the inference endpoint |
| QAS-06 | Scheduled maintenance window | 500 concurrent service activations queued | Provisioning queue + NSO | Planned high-load event | Back pressure applied; activations processed in priority order | No queue overflow; P99 activation latency < 60 s |

### 4.3 Applying QAW Outputs to O&A Architecture

QAW scenarios become the **test oracle** for architectural decisions. For example:

- QAS-01 (5 s RCA response at 80% capacity) → drives the decision to deploy a dedicated inference replica for NOC-facing tools, isolated from batch automation workloads (bulkhead pattern)
- QAS-03 (fallback in 5 s) → mandates that the NSO static template renderer is always warm and never blocked on LLM availability
- QAS-04 (100% reasoning artefacts) → mandates a write-through audit log on every LLM call, not sampled logging

!!! tip "Run QAWs Before Sprint Zero"
    The highest leverage point for a QAW is before the team has committed to technology choices. Once an architecture is in place, QAW outputs become expensive rework rather than cheap decisions. Schedule a QAW at the earliest point that meaningful stakeholders can be assembled.

## 5. Telecom-Specific AI NFRs (GSMA NG.116)

### 5.1 Explainability as a Regulatory Requirement

The CRTC's evolving framework for network management practices (following decisions like Telecom Regulatory Policy 2009-657 and subsequent reviews) increasingly requires that operators be able to demonstrate that automated network management decisions are proportionate, non-discriminatory, and technically necessary. AI-driven decisions that affect traffic routing, service quality, or access to network resources fall within scope.

**NFR**: *Every AI-driven decision that modifies a network element configuration or affects service-level treatment of a subscriber class MUST produce a human-readable explanation logged to a tamper-evident audit store within 500 ms of the decision.*

The explanation must include, at minimum:

- The input context presented to the AI model (sanitised of PII)
- The model version and inference parameters used
- The decision or recommendation produced
- A confidence score or uncertainty estimate
- The human operator or automated policy that authorised execution

### 5.2 Five-Nines Availability for AI-Driven Automation

**NFR**: *The O&A AI automation platform MUST achieve 99.999% availability (≤ 5.26 minutes unplanned downtime per year) for any workflow classified as affecting live customer services. AI components that cannot meet this target MUST be placed in a degraded-but-functional mode, with deterministic fallback paths active.*

This NFR is not asking the LLM inference engine to achieve five-nines — that is generally not achievable for probabilistic inference systems. It is asking the **automation platform as a whole** to achieve five-nines by designing AI components as optional enhancers of a robust deterministic baseline.

The architectural implication is clear: **the deterministic NSO/NETCONF path must work correctly without AI assistance**. AI adds intelligence on top of a resilient foundation; it does not replace the foundation.

### 5.3 Deterministic Latency for Real-Time Decisions

**NFR**: *AI components involved in real-time network decisions (e.g., dynamic traffic steering, fault-triggered rerouting) MUST meet deterministic P99 latency targets. Stochastic variance in LLM inference latency is not acceptable in the real-time path.*

For O&A, real-time decisions (sub-second) must use lightweight, deterministic models (rules engines, classical ML with fixed inference time) rather than LLMs. LLMs are appropriate for **advisory** workflows (RCA generation, config suggestions, documentation) where a human is in the loop. This is a deliberate architectural seam:

| Decision Type | Latency Target | Appropriate AI Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Fault-triggered traffic reroute | < 200 ms | Rules engine / classical ML only |
| SLA breach threshold alert | < 1 s | Statistical anomaly detection |
| Service provisioning (automated) | < 30 s | LLM-assisted with deterministic fallback |
| Config change recommendation | < 60 s | LLM with human approval gate |
| Root-cause analysis report | < 300 s | LLM with full reasoning chain |

### 5.4 Zero-Touch Automation NFRs

GSMA's zero-touch network and service management (ZSM) framework, referenced in NG.116, imposes its own NFR set on autonomous network operations:

- **Closed-loop integrity**: Every closed-loop automation cycle must include a verification step confirming that the intended change was applied and produced the expected telemetry delta.
- **Human escalation path**: Any closed-loop decision with a confidence score below a configurable threshold (default: 0.85) MUST escalate to a human operator rather than auto-applying.
- **Rollback capability**: Every AI-initiated configuration change MUST be reversible within 60 seconds via a single operator action.
- **Audit immutability**: Closed-loop decision logs MUST be written to an append-only store and retained for a minimum of 2 years.

The following table maps each ISO/IEC 25010 quality characteristic to concrete, measurable NFRs for O&A AI systems. Teams should treat this as a starting checklist, refining each NFR through the QAW process with their specific stakeholders.

| ISO Characteristic | Sub-characteristic | O&A AI NFR Example | Measurement Approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Functional Suitability** | Correctness | LLM-generated YANG configs MUST be syntactically valid against the target device's YANG schema 100% of the time before any commit | Automated YANG schema validation in CI; zero schema violations in production |

| **Performance Efficiency** | Time Behaviour | LLM inference pipeline MUST return a YANG config fragment within 8 s at P99 under normal load | Latency histogram in Prometheus; P99 SLO alert |
| **Performance Efficiency** | Resource Utilisation | AI inference workloads MUST NOT consume more than 40% of the NSO host's CPU budget during business hours | Resource quota enforced via Kubernetes + alerting |
| **Performance Efficiency** | Capacity | The provisioning queue MUST handle 1,000 concurrent service orders without degradation | Load test at 1,000 TPS in staging environment |
| **Compatibility** | Interoperability | AI-generated NETCONF edits MUST be compatible with IOS-XR, Junos, and Cisco NSO-managed devices in the O&A device pool | Device compatibility matrix tested in CI against vendor-supplied YANG models |

| **Usability** | Operability | NOC operators MUST be able to review and override any AI-generated config recommendation without specialist AI knowledge | Usability test with NOC staff; override flow completable in < 3 clicks |

| **Reliability** | Availability | LLM inference pipeline MUST recover from a model timeout within 500 ms by routing to the fallback deterministic path | Chaos engineering test: inject 100% timeout; verify fallback activates within 500 ms |
| **Reliability** | Fault Tolerance | The provisioning platform MUST continue processing queued service orders when the AI reasoning engine is unavailable, using static NSO templates | Fault injection test: kill inference pod; verify queue drains via fallback |
| **Reliability** | Recoverability | Following an NSO CDB corruption event, the AI audit log MUST provide sufficient information to reconstruct the last 1,000 AI-driven config changes | Disaster recovery test: restore CDB from audit log replay |
| **Security** | Integrity | YANG configs generated by AI MUST be digitally signed before submission to NSO, and the signature MUST be verified before commit | Signature verification step in NETCONF commit pipeline; signature failure = hard reject |
| **Security** | Confidentiality | Subscriber network telemetry used as LLM context MUST be tokenised/pseudonymised; raw subscriber identifiers MUST NOT appear in LLM prompts | Data pipeline audit; automated PII scanner on LLM prompt payloads |
| **Security** | Non-repudiation | Every AI-initiated NETCONF commit MUST carry a traceable identity (service account + reasoning artefact ID) in the NETCONF `edit-config` metadata | NETCONF session audit; every commit has a linked AI decision record |
| **Maintainability** | Analysability | YANG-generated configs MUST be human-readable and interpretable by a network engineer without AI tooling | Peer review checklist: network engineer can explain config intent without AI assistance |

| **Maintainability** | Testability | Each AI pipeline step MUST be independently unit-testable with deterministic mock inputs and outputs | Unit test coverage ≥ 80% for each pipeline component; no tests that require live LLM |
| **Portability** | Adaptability | The O&A AI platform MUST deploy on-premises (Rogers data centres) and on hybrid cloud without code changes | Same container images deployed to on-prem and cloud staging; tests pass in both |
| **Portability** | Replaceability | The NSO service template renderer MUST be operable as a standalone component, independent of AI availability | Standalone integration test with AI components disabled |

## 7. The Explainability Gap

### 7.1 Why Telecom Regulators Require Explainable AI

Network operators occupy a uniquely regulated position: they are essential infrastructure providers, subject to non-discrimination obligations, open-access requirements, and in Canada, CRTC oversight of network management practices. When AI makes decisions about how traffic flows, how faults are triaged, or how SLA commitments are met, regulators need to be able to assess whether those decisions comply with the operator's licence conditions.

The **explainability gap** is the distance between what an AI system does and what a human can understand about why it did it. For classical deterministic systems (rules engines, explicit state machines), this gap is zero — you can read the code. For modern LLMs and deep learning systems, the gap can be substantial.

In telecom, the explainability gap creates three distinct risk categories:

1. **Regulatory risk**: An AI-driven traffic management decision that cannot be explained may be presumed to be discriminatory by a regulator, even if it is not.
2. **Operational risk**: NOC operators who cannot understand why the AI made a change cannot confidently assess whether to roll it back during an incident.
3. **Liability risk**: If an AI-driven misconfiguration causes a service outage, an operator needs an audit trail demonstrating due diligence — that the AI was acting within approved parameters and that human oversight was in place.

### 7.2 Fault Routing and SLA Management as Explainability Test Cases

Two O&A workflows serve as practical test cases for explainability requirements:

**AI-assisted fault routing**: When an AI agent analyses network telemetry and routes a fault ticket to a specific remediation playbook (e.g., "apply MPLS FRR bypass on PE-Router-YYZ-03"), the decision must be explainable in terms that a network engineer can verify: which alarms triggered the decision, which topology data was consulted, and why this remediation was preferred over alternatives.

**AI-assisted SLA management**: When an AI system adjusts QoS markings or traffic engineering tunnels to protect an SLA for a specific enterprise customer, the decision must be explainable in terms of the SLA parameters being protected and the impact (if any) on other traffic classes.

### 7.3 The Structured Explanation Pattern for O&A

A practical approach to closing the explainability gap in O&A is to require every AI decision to produce a **structured explanation artefact** alongside its output. This artefact is stored in the audit log (see QAS-04) and is separate from the AI model's internal chain-of-thought (which may not be human-readable).

```yaml
# Example structured explanation artefact (YAML)
# Stored in the O&A audit log for every AI-driven NETCONF commit

decision_id: "ai-decision-2024-11-15T14:32:07Z-a3f9"
workflow: "l3vpn-auto-provisioning"
model_version: "noa-config-gen-v2.1"
timestamp: "2024-11-15T14:32:07Z"
operator_authorised_by: "nso-automation-svc@rogers.com"

inputs_summary:
  service_order_id: "TMF641-ORD-00042817"
  customer_segment: "enterprise"
  requested_bandwidth_mbps: 1000
  topology_nodes_consulted: ["PE-YYZ-01", "PE-YYZ-03", "P-YYZ-02"]

decision:
  action: "generate_l3vpn_yang_config"
  config_artefact_id: "yang-cfg-2024-11-15-a3f9"
  confidence_score: 0.91

rationale_summary: >
  Selected PE-YYZ-01 as ingress PE based on available BGP-free core
  capacity (87% headroom) and shortest IGP path to CE. Rejected
  PE-YYZ-03 due to active maintenance window (scheduled until 16:00).

fallback_used: false
schema_validation_passed: true
human_review_required: false   # confidence_score >= 0.85 threshold
```

!!! note "Structured Explanations Are Not Model Interpretability"
    Structured explanation artefacts document *what decision was made and on what inputs*, not *how the model arrived at the decision internally*. This is intentional: the goal is **operational accountability**, not full model interpretability (which remains an open research problem for large language models). Operational accountability is what regulators and NOC operators actually need.

## 8. Practical NFR Checklist for O&A AI Features

Use this checklist when writing acceptance criteria for any AI feature on the O&A backlog. Each category maps to the frameworks discussed in this document.

### Stability & Resilience (Nygard)

- [ ] LLM inference calls are wrapped in a circuit breaker with defined open/half-open thresholds
- [ ] Every integration point has an explicit timeout (no unbounded waits)
- [ ] AI components are isolated in separate resource pools from deterministic automation paths (bulkhead)
- [ ] Back pressure is implemented on all inbound event queues with a defined high-water mark
- [ ] A deterministic fallback path exists and is tested independently of AI availability
- [ ] Fail-fast input validation rejects malformed service orders before any NETCONF session opens
- [ ] Steady-state log/queue purging is configured to prevent unbounded growth

### Quality Attribute Scenarios (BCK / QAW)

- [ ] A quality attribute scenario (6-part format) has been written for each significant NFR
- [ ] QAW has been conducted with NOC, architecture, security, and compliance stakeholders
- [ ] Top QAW scenarios are tracked as acceptance criteria in the team backlog
- [ ] Architectural decisions are traceable to specific quality attribute scenarios

### Telecom AI Governance (GSMA NG.116)

- [ ] Explainability: every AI-driven config change produces a structured explanation artefact
- [ ] Availability: AI platform availability target is defined and the fallback path can meet it independently
- [ ] Latency: real-time decisions (< 1 s) do NOT use LLMs; LLMs are bounded to advisory/async workflows
- [ ] Fairness: AI traffic management recommendations are auditable for non-discrimination
- [ ] Energy: LLM inference workload has a defined carbon/compute budget per automation job
- [ ] Human oversight: confidence thresholds are configured; low-confidence decisions escalate to humans
- [ ] Rollback: every AI-initiated config change is reversible within 60 seconds

### NFR Taxonomy (ISO/IEC 25010)

- [ ] **Functional Suitability**: AI output is schema-validated before any system action
- [ ] **Performance Efficiency**: P99 latency targets are defined per workflow step
- [ ] **Compatibility**: AI-generated configs are tested against all device types in the O&A device pool
- [ ] **Usability**: NOC operators can override AI decisions without specialist AI knowledge
- [ ] **Reliability**: Chaos/fault injection tests verify fallback behaviour
- [ ] **Security**: LLM prompts are scanned for PII; outputs are signed before commit
- [ ] **Maintainability**: AI pipeline components are independently unit-testable with mock LLMs
- [ ] **Portability**: Platform deploys identically on-premises and on hybrid cloud

### Auditability & Compliance

- [ ] Audit log is append-only and tamper-evident
- [ ] Every AI decision is linked to a reasoning artefact retained for ≥ 2 years
- [ ] Audit log can reconstruct the last 1,000 AI-driven config changes
- [ ] AI decision records include model version, confidence score, and authorising identity
- [ ] Prompt injection detection is active on all AI input surfaces
- [ ] Regulatory explainability report can be generated on demand (< 1 business day)
